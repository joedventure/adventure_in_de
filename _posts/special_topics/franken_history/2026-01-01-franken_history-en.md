Germany currently consists of 16 federal states (Bundesland), with Bavaria being the largest in terms of area. Many of our stereotypes and cultural perceptions of Germany originate from here, such as Oktoberfest, the traditional Bavarian dress (Dirndl), and roasted pork knuckle. However, for some people living in Bavaria, their cultural identity does not lie with Bavaria, but rather with Franconia (known as Franken in German), a region not as commonly heard of by outsiders. I remember telling a German friend that I was going to Bavaria for a trip; he asked if I was going to Munich. When I replied "Nuremberg," I was immediately corrected that it wasn't Bavaria 😂.

Franconia is located in three administrative regions in the north of Bavaria: Upper Franconia (Oberfranken), Middle Franconia (Mittelfranken), and Lower Franconia (Unterfranken). In earlier times, Franconia covered northern Bavaria as well as parts of Hesse, Thuringia, and Baden-Württemberg. It includes many famous cities such as Würzburg, Bamberg, and Nuremberg (Nürnberg). Additionally, the cities of Ansbach and Coburg hold special historical significance for the Duchy of Prussia (Herzogtum Preußen) and the current British Royal Family.

Next, let us travel back to the Middle Ages to learn about the past of Franconia.

## Ancient Times
![Franken-1]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/franken-1.png)
<sup><sub>The Frankish Kingdom in the mid-5th century<br>Excerpt from Droysen, Allgemeiner historischer Handatlas (Bielefeld und Leipzig: Verlag von Velhagen & Klasing, 1886)<sup><sub>

Traces of Celtic (Kelten) settlement in the Franconian region date back to the Bronze Age around 800 BC. According to records, during the Roman Empire, this Main River basin was home to Germanic tribes, including the Marcomanni (Markomannen), who migrated eastward after being repelled by the Roman Empire between the 7th and 3rd centuries BC, and later the Hermunduri (Hermunduren).

By the beginning of the 1st century AD, the southern part of Franconia was incorporated into the Roman province of Raetia. During the reign of Augustus (born Gaius Octavius Thurinus, 63 BC-14 AD), the first emperor of the Roman Empire, Roman military camps, the Upper Germanic-Rhaetian Limes (Limes Germanicus), and forts such as Kastell Biriciana bei Weißenburg were constructed. Under constant harassment from northern Germanic tribes, the Alamanni (Alemannen) occupied the region around 250 AD. During this same period, China was in the Three Kingdoms era.

As for northern Franconia, it was occupied by the Franks (Franken) between the 3rd and 5th centuries AD. The Franks were originally a Germanic people living near the lower Rhine. Their name did not appear until around the 3rd century AD, though the original Frankish tribes were already well known to the Romans. Generally, historians divide the Franks living along the Rhine on the northern border of the Roman Empire into two groups: the Salian Franks (Saliens) on the west bank and the Ripuarian Franks (Ripuarier) on the east bank, also known as Rhine Franks (Rheinfranken). It is worth mentioning that the Ripuarian Franks once conquered the Roman frontier city of Cologne (Köln). There are few written records of the Ripuarian Franks; they appeared as part of Austrasia (Austrasien), the core region of the Merovingian dynasty (Merowinger) of the Frankish Kingdom. This region covered present-day northern Franconia and extended to the lower Rhine. This period coincides with the Wei, Jin, and Northern and Southern dynasties in China.

## The Rise of the Franks and the Merovingian Dynasty
![Franken-2]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/franken-2.png)
<sup><sub>The Frankish Kingdom, 511 AD.<br>Excerpted from Droysen, Allgemeiner historischer Handatlas (Bielefeld und Leipzig: Verlag von Velhagen & Klasing, 1886)<sup><sub>

Originating from the lower reaches of the Rhine, the Salian Franks settled in northeastern Gaul (now Tournai, Belgium) within Roman territory after being defeated by Rome in 358 AD. Records indicate that in 481 AD, Clovis I, the leader of the Salian Franks, rose to kingship after conquering several neighboring Frankish tribes, marking the official debut of the Merovingian Dynasty in history.

In the following years, Clovis I continued his outward expansion:

- In 486 AD, he defeated the Kingdom of Soissons, the last vestige of Roman power in Gaul.

- In 496 AD, he emerged victorious at the Battle of Tolbiac (Zülpich), bringing the modern-day Franconia region under Frankish control.

- In 507 AD, he defeated the Visigothic army at the Battle of Vouillé.

By this point, the Frankish Kingdom had unified nearly all of Gaul and moved its capital to Paris. It is also worth noting that, under the persuasion of his wife, Clovis I began practicing Catholicism around 500 AD. This not only garnered more support for the Frankish Kingdom but also signaled Europe’s gradual transition from polytheism to monotheism.

Clovis I passed away in Paris in 511 AD and was interred at the Basilica of Saint-Denis. According to tradition, the Frankish Kingdom was divided among his four sons, forming four independent sub-kingdoms centered in Metz (including present-day Franconia), Orléans, Paris, and Soissons.

## Austrasia — The Cradle of the Carolingian Dynasty
Austrasia encompassed the modern-day lower Rhine, Belgium, northeastern France, and Franconia, which then served merely as a frontier zone. Around 511 AD, Austrasia was actually known as the Kingdom of Metz (Reich von Metz), inherited by Theuderic I, the eldest son of Clovis I. Under his rule, Austrasia continued to expand eastward, incorporating the Kingdom of the Thuringians in 531 AD. The name "Austrasia" first appeared in literature around 580 AD, mentioned by Gregory of Tours.

To the west of Austrasia lay Neustria, centered in Soissons. After 511 AD, it was inherited by Chlothar I, the youngest son of Clovis I. An ambitious monarch, he annexed the other three kingdoms during his reign and incorporated the Kingdom of the Burgundians (Burgundenreich) to the south in 534 AD.

The Frankish Kingdom fractured again after Chlothar I's death; however, his grandson, Chlothar II, successfully followed in his footsteps to reunify the kingdom in 613 AD. This time, he had to make concessions to the Frankish nobility who had supported his campaigns. In 614 AD, Chlothar II promulgated the Edict of Paris, delegating more power to local nobles and strengthening their influence within the Frankish Kingdom. In 623 AD, under pressure from the Austrasian nobility, Chlothar II appointed his son, Dagobert I, as King of Austrasia and named Arnulf of Metz and Pippin of Landen as Mayors of the Palace (Hausmeier) to assist in its governance.

Following the death of his father in 629 AD, Dagobert I ascended the throne with noble support and quickly demonstrated his political and military prowess. Under his leadership, the Frankish Kingdom flourished, expanding its territory and accumulating wealth. At the transport hub of Würzburg in Franconia, Dagobert I appointed Radulf as Duke of Thuringia/Franconia. During this period, many missionaries, such as Saint Kilian and his companions, arrived to preach in the region. Later, the Duke of Franconia, Gosbert, also converted to Christianity.

Over time, the capital of the Frankish Kingdom moved to Paris, becoming the power center once again. This marginalized Dagobert I's old stronghold of Austrasia, leading to resentment among the local nobility. Meanwhile, frequent Slavic incursions and raids on the eastern border created a tense security situation. In 631 AD, Dagobert I sought to strengthen the eastern border through military force, aiming to boost his prestige in Austrasia and consolidate his rule. Unfortunately, the military campaign ended in failure, and the dissatisfaction of the Austrasian nobles erupted. Under pressure, he appointed his three-year-old son, Sigebert III, as King of Austrasia to appease the eastern nobility. The cycle of history repeated itself.

## The Rise of the Carolingian Dynasty
After the death of Pippin of Landen in 640 AD, Grimoald the Elder (Grimoald der Ältere) succeeded to the position his father once held after a period of consolidation. During this time, he persuaded King Sigebert III of the Duchy of Austrasia to adopt his son, Childebert the Adopted (Childebert der Adoptierte), who then inherited the Austrasian throne upon Sigebert III's death.

Fast forward to Pippin of Herstal (Pippin der Mittlere), the grandson of Pippin of Landen and Arnulf, Bishop of Metz. He was the first scion and heir of the union between these two powerful families. In 687, at the Battle of Tertry (Schlacht bei Tertry), he successfully defeated the Frankish King Theuderic III (Theuderich III) and his allied forces from Neustria-Burgundy to the west, subsequently styling himself as the Duke of the Franks. This marked Austrasia's dominance within the Frankish Kingdom, a status that would persist until the end of the Merovingian era.

Following the death of Pippin of Herstal in 714, his last direct heir—his eight-year-old grandson—was appointed Mayor of the Palace. Meanwhile, Pippin’s illegitimate son, Charles "The Hammer" (Karl Martell), was imprisoned in Cologne by Pippin’s legitimate wife, Plectrude (Plektrudis). In 715, civil war erupted within the Frankish Kingdom. Nobles from the Duchy of Neustria launched an attack on Austrasia, forcing Plectrude to flee to Cologne. Charles escaped captivity and gained the support of the Austrasian nobility. Ultimately, he repelled the Neustrian forces at the Battle of Vincy (Schlacht von Vincy bei Cambrai) in 717 and the Battle of Soissons in 718, succeeding as the Mayor of the Palace of Austrasia and Duke of the Franks.

From 718 to 739, Charles conducted numerous military campaigns, including expeditions against the Saxons (Sachsen) in 718, 720, and 738 to curb their incursions; the conquest of the Frisians (Friesen) in 719, successfully expanding the territory; the integration of Alemannia into the Frankish Empire; the peaceful diplomatic integration of Thuringia and the Franks of the Main region; and intervention in Bavarian power struggles to seat an ally as ruler. Most significant was his defense against Muslim forces at the Battle of Tours in 732, which consolidated the southwestern border of the Frankish Empire and was credited with "saving Christianity." These campaigns laid the foundation for the subsequent Carolingian Dynasty.

In 737, King Theuderic IV (Theuderic IV) passed away, and Charles became the sole ruler of the empire. Before his death in 741, Charles divided the Frankish Kingdom between his two sons, Carloman (Karlmann) and Pippin the Short (Pippin der Kleine), making them Mayors of the Palace for Austrasia and Neustria, respectively. Shortly after, their half-brother Grifo revolted. To bolster their legitimacy, Carloman and Pippin the Short decided to restore a Merovingian bloodline, Childeric III (Childerich III), as the Frankish King in 743, though he held no real power. In 747, Carloman retired to become a monk; in 751, Pippin the Short, with the support of the Frankish nobility, deposed Childeric III and overthrew the Merovingian Dynasty. Thus, the Carolingian Dynasty officially entered the stage of history.

Legend has it that Saint Boniface (Bonifatius), then Archbishop of Mainz, crowned Pippin the Short in 751 and donated vast lands to the Bishop of Würzburg in 752. The first Bishop of Würzburg was Burchard, recommended by Saint Boniface and appointed by the Pope in 742. Between 750 and 751, Burchard traveled to Rome on behalf of Pippin the Short to seek papal support. This illustrates the deep connection between the Frankish Kingdom and its eastern territories, the present-day region of Franconia.

In 754, the Pope personally appealed to Pippin for help. Pippin marched into Italy in 754 and captured the Lombard king in 756. Subsequently, a vast territory stretching from Ravenna to Rome was designated as papal jurisdiction (the Donation of Pippin), forming the prototype of the Papal States and further establishing the dominance of the Carolingian Dynasty.

## Charlemagne and the Prototype of Modern Western Europe
Charlemagne (Karl der Große, French: Charlemagne) was the son of Pippin the Short. During his 45-year reign (768–814), he not only successfully expanded the borders of the Frankish Kingdom but also revitalized Western European culture and political order, earning him the title "Father of Europe."
When Charlemagne inherited the Frankish Kingdom in 768, he co-ruled with his brother, Carloman I. Upon Carloman's sudden death in 771, Charlemagne became the sole Frankish King and began his campaigns of expansion, including:

1. The Lombard War (773-774): Charlemagne defeated the Kingdom of the Lombards in northern Italy, establishing Frankish control over Italy and declaring himself King of the Lombards in 774.

2. Incorporation of the Kingdom of Bavaria: After taking over Carloman I's territories in 771 and intervening in Italy in 773-74, only the Kingdom of Bavaria remained outside the Frankish fold. It was then ruled by Tassilo III (Tassilo III). Though he had once pledged fealty to Pippin the Short, he gradually sought independent royal status in Bavaria.

In 787, Charlemagne summoned Tassilo to Worms and demanded his submission. Despite Tassilo's attempts at papal mediation, a lack of internal support and the defection of Bavarian nobles to the Frankish side forced Tassilo to swear fealty in 787. However, in 788, Tassilo was accused of conspiring with the Avars (Awaren) and desertion; he was sentenced to death at Ingelheim, though Charlemagne commuted it to life imprisonment. In 794, at the Council of Frankfurt, Tassilo publicly confessed and renounced all rights. Modern scholars view these charges as a calculated political trial by Charlemagne to eliminate Tassilo’s threat and end Bavarian independence. Nevertheless, Bavaria retained some distinctiveness in ecclesiastical and administrative matters while being fully incorporated into the empire.

3. The Frankish Kingdom and the Avar Kingdom to the Southeast: The Avars were nomadic horsemen from the Asian steppes who established a powerful kingdom in the Balkans in the early 7th century. In 788, they invaded the Frankish Kingdom, targeting northern Italy and Bavaria. These incursions may have been prompted by appeals from Frankish opposition or Avar fears of becoming the next target of Charlemagne's expansion.

In 791, Charlemagne launched a massive invasion that forced the Avars to retreat. This conflict is described as Charlemagne's largest war aside from the Saxon Wars. The Avar War held significant symbolic value as a crusade against "pagans," allowing Charlemagne to cast himself as a Christian ruler. Further attacks were planned but delayed by the Saxon revolt. In 795, envoys from an Avar faction appeared at the Elbe, offering submission to Charlemagne as their suzerain; they were baptized the following year.

In 796, Frankish forces invaded the Avar Kingdom again, the new Khagan surrendered, and Avar power was thoroughly crushed. The Franks then began incorporating border regions directly into the empire, advancing Christianization and establishing new Marches to guard against the rising Bulgarians in the Balkans.

4. The Saxon Wars (c. 772-804): This thirty-year conflict was one of Charlemagne's primary military endeavors. Since the time of his grandfather, the Franks had clashed with the Saxons to the northeast. In 772, following a Saxon raid on Deventer, the Franks marched from Worms to Eresburg and destroyed the Irminsul, the "world tree" of Germanic mythology. After multiple expeditions, a turning point came in 785 when the Saxon leader Widukind finally submitted and was baptized. Sporadic resistance continued in some tribes until finally subsiding around 804.

During the Saxon Wars, Charlemagne relocated many Franks to the region to suppress resistance and established a series of dioceses to consolidate control and convert the Saxons from paganism to Christianity—namely Paderborn, Münster, Bremen, Minden, Verden, and Osnabrück. Notably, the coat of arms of modern Lower Saxony is said to derive from Widukind’s black horse banner. After his conversion, Charlemagne reportedly gifted him a white horse, leading to the current white horse on a red background.

5. Spain: In 777, a request from Arab envoys from the Iberian Peninsula provided Charlemagne the opportunity to expand Frankish influence into Iberia while acting as a defender of Christianity. In 778, due to the Saxon revolt, he was forced to retreat. During the withdrawal, the Frankish army was ambushed at the Roncevaux Pass, suffering heavy losses including the death of Count Roland (the subject of the Song of Roland). In later attempts, the Frankish Kingdom successfully established the Spanish March (Spanische Mark) in northern Spain in 795 and captured Barcelona in 801.

Pope Leo III, after assuming the papacy in 795, immediately faced fierce opposition from political rivals and was violently attacked in 799, leading him to seek Charlemagne's aid in Paderborn. Meanwhile, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire) still held significant influence in Europe, especially Italy. In 797, Empress Irene usurped the throne from her son. As a woman, Irene faced domestic opposition in Constantinople. These factors gave Leo III an opportunity to declare the Roman throne vacant and crown Charlemagne, thereby elevating his own status and securing a powerful ally. Consequently, on Christmas Day 800 AD, Pope Leo III crowned Charlemagne Emperor (Kaiser). This made Charlemagne the first Western Emperor since the deposition of the last Roman Emperor in 476. The event marked a turning point in European history, establishing Charlemagne as the most authoritative leader in the West with military, secular, and religious legitimacy, and cementing the concept of the "Alliance between Pope and Emperor." However, it also signaled a deeper rupture between Western Europe and the Byzantine Empire.

By this point, the Frankish Kingdom encompassed modern France, Germany, Italy, and parts of Spain and Central Europe. Charlemagne adopted a governance model combining central authority with local autonomy, appointing Counts and Margraves to manage regions. Legally, he unified the legal system and emphasized judicial fairness. Economically, he promoted agriculture, unified the currency, and encouraged trade to stabilize the nation's foundation.

Religion and culture were also pillars of his rule. Working closely with the Pope, he promoted Christianity nationwide and revived education through the "Carolingian Renaissance." He established schools and encouraged the study of Latin and classical literature, laying the groundwork for future European cultural development.

Charlemagne’s reign combined strong central power, a solid economic base, and a broad cultural revival, contributing immensely to the civilization of Medieval Europe.

After his death in 814 in Aachen (Aachen), the kingdom passed to his son, Louis the Pious (Ludwig der Fromme). In 843, the empire was split again into three parts: Louis the German (Ludwig der Deutsche) received East Francia, which would become the Holy Roman Empire; Charles the Bald (Karl der Kahle) received West Francia, which would become the Kingdom of France; and Lothair I (Lothar I) received Middle Francia, covering parts of the present-day Netherlands, Belgium, Switzerland, and northern Italy. Middle Francia was absorbed by its neighbors in subsequent years. Thus, the prototype of modern Western Europe was born.

After Dagobert I's death in 639 AD, Pippin of Landen returned to assist Sigebert III. From then on, the actual power of the Merovingian Dynasty gradually fell into the hands of the Mayors of the Palace from the families of Arnulf of Metz and Pippin of Landen. This transfer of power eventually facilitated the rise of the Carolingian Dynasty. Consequently, Dagobert I is often regarded as the last Merovingian monarch to wield real power.
