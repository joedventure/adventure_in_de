## History of Heppenheim
Heppenheim an der Bergstraße Heppenheim is the fourth largest city in the Bergstraße district of Hesse, with approximately 28,000 residents. It is also well-known as the hometown of the German Formula One champion, Sebastian Vettel.

The Heppenheim area has been inhabited since the Neolithic period. In the 1st century AD, the Romans brought Heppenheim under their rule, constructing estates and a sophisticated road network. However, with the decline of the Roman Empire, Roman territories east of the Rhine were gradually abandoned. By the mid-5th century, the Franks crossed the Rhine for the first time, and by the end of the 7th century, they had solidified their rule over the region, laying a deep foundation for its development.

Heppenheim and St. Peter's Church in the city center were first mentioned on July 17, 755, in a donation document within the Lorsch Codex. Urbanization began gradually from the 11th century. Although the specific documents granting Heppenheim city status have been lost, the city seal from the early 14th century indicates that it already possessed the scale and rights of a city at that time. To protect the market and its citizens, a city wall with three gates was erected around the settlement.

In 1065, King Henry IV granted Lorsch Abbey to Adalbert, Archbishop of Bremen, sparking a territorial dispute. To protect the abbey's property, Abbot Udalrich built Starkenburg Castle on a hill overlooking Heppenheim and successfully repelled Adalbert’s siege in 1066. In 1232, following the decline of the abbey, Heppenheim and Starkenburg were transferred to the jurisdiction of the Archbishopric of Mainz. Between 1461 and 1623, Heppenheim and the surrounding region were ruled by the Electoral Palatinate. From 1803, the land was incorporated into the Landgraviate of Hesse-Darmstadt, eventually becoming part of the modern German state of Hesse.

Heppenheim also played a significant role in the 19th-century German unification movement. On October 10, 1847, representatives from five member states of the German Confederation gathered here at the invitation of politician David Hansemann to discuss German unity, paving the way for the Frankfurt Assembly of 1848.

Today, Heppenheim is a popular tourist destination, beloved for its charming Old Town, the spectacular Starkenburg Castle, and its long-standing wine culture.

## St. Peter's Church (St. Peter)
![St. Peter]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Heppenheim/Heppenheim_St_Peter.jpg)
The St. Peter's Church we see in Heppenheim today was built between 1901 and 1904. However, like many cathedrals elsewhere, its origins can be traced back to much older times. Records indicate that a castle existed here in 755 AD, extending to today’s Market Square, with the predecessor of St. Peter's Church included within its bounds. Between 773 and 1232, the church belonged to the parish of Lorsch Abbey, and numerous renovations took place during this period.

In 1693, the church suffered severe damage due to the French invasion. Consequently, a large-scale restoration project began in 1698, adding a new nave and galleries to the western side. In 1732, a fire necessitated the reconstruction of the roof. In 1884, the decision was made to rebuild the church, a building committee was established, and the foundation stone was finally laid in 1901.

## Market Square of Heppenheim
![HP_Rathaus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Heppenheim/Heppenheim_Rathaus.jpg)
The Market Square (Marktplatz) is the heart of the Old Town, surrounded by traditional German half-timbered buildings. Among the most iconic are the Baroque-style Town Hall (Rathaus) and the Old Pirsch Pharmacy (Apotheke Pirsch) located opposite.

Between 1817 and 1818, the famous chemist Justus von Liebig spent ten months here as an apprentice. He is renowned for inventing beef extract, mineral fertilizer, and baking powder, earning him the title "Father of the Fertilizer Industry."

## Electoral Mainz Administrative Court (Kurmeinzer Amtshof)
![Kurmeinzer_Amtshof]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Heppenheim/Heppenheim_Kurmeinzer_Amtshof.jpg)
In the center of Heppenheim lies the expansive complex of the Kurmeinzer Amtshof. It is believed to have been built after the great city fire of 1369. The oldest part is the main building on the south side, which houses the Electoral Hall (Kurfürstensaal) decorated with Gothic frescoes. Many solemn state events were held here, such as the 1435 ceremony where Dietrich von Erbach, Archbishop of Mainz, Elector, and Chancellor of the Holy Roman Empire, swore his oath of allegiance to the Emperor. Additionally, the Free Democratic Party (FDP) of Germany was founded here in 1948.

Today, the complex houses the Museum of Local History and Folklore, an East German cultural exhibition hall, and multi-functional event spaces. Every summer, visitors can attend the Heppenheim Festival (Heppenheimer Festspiele) to enjoy brilliant theatrical performances while savoring the renowned Bergstraße wines, experiencing a unique summer atmosphere.

## Heppenheim Lantern Path (Laternenweg)
![Lamp]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Heppenheim/Heppenheim_Lamp.jpg)
Strolling through the alleys of Heppenheim, you will notice that the streetlights here are different and full of artistic flair. This street, known as the Lantern Path (Laternenweg), features about 150 lanterns decorated with exquisite paper-cut art.

Each lamp features a different pattern, representing various fairy tales from Hesse. Brief summaries of the stories are attached below the lanterns. Walking along the path feels like traveling through different worlds of folklore.

## Starkenburg Castle (Starkenburg)
![Heppenheim_Starkenburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Heppenheim/Heppenheim_Starkenburg.jpg)
In 1065, for defensive purposes, Lorsch Abbey built Starkenburg Castle on the Schlossberg at an altitude of 295 meters. Originally, the castle consisted of wooden structures, resembling a Roman fort. After 1232, under the rule of the Electors of Mainz, the castle was transformed into a medieval fortress and further expanded between 1675 and 1689 into a stronghold combining defensive and economic functions.

After the Mainz garrison withdrew in 1765, the castle gradually fell into ruin. It wasn't until the 19th century that municipal authorities delayed its destruction by banning the demolition of walls and the removal of stones, with restoration work beginning in 1877. In 1904, because the main tower was in danger of collapsing, the authorities decided to demolish it and build a new one in the following years. However, the new tower was not placed in the center of the original inner courtyard but was moved to the western side of the castle to preserve a larger open courtyard space.

Today, Starkenburg is not only a historical site but also houses a youth hostel with 121 beds and 5 meeting rooms, allowing travelers to enjoy a unique stay within the historical atmosphere of this ancient castle.
