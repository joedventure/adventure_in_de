## History of Meißen
![Albrechtsburg]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Meißen/Meißen.jpg)

Meißen, a small town located near Dresden, can be considered the cradle of the modern Free State of Saxony. The Saxony we know today actually shares no direct lineage with the Duchy of Saxony (Stammesherzogtum Sachsen), which was one of the five tribal duchies of Germany in the 9th century. That original duchy was situated in northern Germany, whereas the contemporary state of Saxony lies along the upper reaches of the Elbe River.

During that era, the Thuringians were the most influential power in this region. At the beginning of the 5th century, the Thuringians began expanding their empire into central Germany with the support of the Huns under Attila. Following the collapse of the Huns' empire in the latter half of the 5th century, they established their own independent Kingdom of Thuringia.

In 531 AD, Thuringia was defeated by the Franks (Schlacht an der Unstrut). The southern part of the Thuringian kingdom came under the rule of the Frankish Empire, while the northern part was governed by the Duchy of Saxony. The area east of the Saale River, including the territory of present-day Saxony, was controlled and settled by the Slavic Sorbs at the end of the 6th century.

Between 929 and 931 AD, Henry the Fowler (Heinrich I), King of East Francia, conquered the area and built the castle "Burg Misnia." In 968 AD, the Diocese of Meißen was established under the jurisdiction of the Bishop of Meißen. The Margraviate of Meißen (Markgrafschaft Meißen) was also founded in the same year, serving as the foremost frontier for the subsequent German eastward expansion (Ostsiedlung).

Due to the city's profound significance to Saxon history, the Free State of Saxony chose Albrechtsburg Castle in Meißen as the site to officially re-establish the state on October 3, 1990—the day of German Reunification.
