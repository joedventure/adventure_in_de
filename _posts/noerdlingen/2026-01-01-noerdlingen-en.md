## History of Nördlingen
Nördlingen, much like Rothenburg ob der Tauber, boasts a fully preserved city wall and remains one of the few towns that still retain its medieval character.

Located approximately 80 kilometers southeast of Rothenburg, the earliest documentary mention of Nördlingen dates back to 898 AD, when it was part of the Carolingian Dynasty of the Frankish Empire. Even earlier, around 85 AD, this area belonged to the Raetia province of the Roman Empire before being occupied by the Alemanni in the 3rd century.

In 1215, Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II granted Nördlingen city rights, marking its official establishment as a town. In the same year, construction of the city walls began, providing a solid defense for the settlement. Benefiting from its location at the intersection of two major trade routes—Frankfurt to Würzburg and Augsburg/Nuremberg to Ulm—Nördlingen rapidly evolved into a prosperous commercial hub. However, with the outbreak of the Thirty Years' War, the city became a battlefield twice, in 1634 and 1645, and suffered further damage during the War of the Spanish Succession in the early 18th century. These conflicts caused Nördlingen's commercial status to decline; it was not until 1939 that its population returned to 1618 levels.

The geographical location of Nördlingen is also unique. The town is situated within a crater formed by a meteorite impact approximately 15 million years ago, about 6 kilometers from the center of the impact. The immense pressure and high temperatures of the impact created countless microscopic diamonds within the local rocks. If you have the chance to examine the local stones closely, you might just discover these naturally formed tiny diamonds.

In addition, the town's architectural style is deeply impressive, especially its orange-red roofs and nearly circular city walls. Fans of "Attack on Titan" will likely find the scenery strikingly familiar.
