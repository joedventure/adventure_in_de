## History of Dresden
![Frauenkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Frauenkirche.jpg)
![Oper]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Semperoper.jpg)
Dresden is likely a familiar name to many travelers. As early as the Neolithic period, tribes of the Sorbs (a branch of the Slavs) lived on the north bank of the Elbe, calling the area "Drežany," meaning "forest dwellers by the river."

Dresden was first mentioned in historical documents in 1206. During the German eastward expansion (Ostsiedlung), the Margrave of Meissen chose to establish a German town on the south bank of the Elbe, naming it Civitas Dresdene, which laid the foundation for the city's development. Over the following centuries, it evolved into an electoral and royal residence and became the capital of the Kingdom of Saxony. Known for its prosperous history, vibrant arts and culture, and exquisite Baroque architecture, the city is often called "Florence on the Elbe."

Thanks to its deep cultural roots and abundance of scientific talent, the microelectronics industry—long-established in the region—was the first to recover after World War II. Today, the area is also known as "Silicon Saxony."

Dresden's most famous landmark is undoubtedly the Church of Our Lady (Frauenkirche). It features one of the largest domes in Europe and is considered an outstanding example of Protestant sacred architecture. It was originally built as a symbol of the Dresden citizens' commitment to Protestantism after their ruler converted to Catholicism. After being destroyed by Allied air raids in WWII, reconstruction did not begin until after the reunification of East and West Germany in 1994. Today, the Frauenkirche stands as a symbol of reconciliation between former enemies.

## Procession of Princes (Fürstenzug)
![Fürstenzug]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Fürstenzug.jpg)
The Procession of Princes was conceived by the German artist and professor at the Dresden Academy of Fine Arts, Wilhelm Walther, to celebrate the 800th anniversary of the House of Wettin, the rulers of Saxony.

This magnificent work was originally completed in 1873 as a sgraffito mural. Due to damage caused by natural weathering, the mural was replaced with Meissen porcelain tiles between 1904 and 1907. It is now the largest porcelain artwork in the world, stretching 102 meters in length and consisting of approximately 23,000 exquisite tiles.

This spectacular procession brings 94 figures to life, depicting 34 margraves, dukes, prince-electors, and kings of the Kingdom of Saxony since 1127. At the end of the procession is a self-portrait of the artist Wilhelm Walther, eternally marking his place in this long river of glorious history.

## Pfunds Dairy (Pfunds Molkerei)
![Molkerei]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Molkerei.jpg)
Paul Gustav Leander Pfund was a farmer from Reinholdshain, near Dresden. In 1879, he realized his vision of providing fresh and hygienic milk to Dresden by opening a shop at Neustädter Waldgasse 1 (now Görlitzer Straße), where customers could witness the entire process from cow to counter.

Initially, the company sold about 150 liters of milk daily, which increased to 60,000 liters by the 1930s, using pasteurization to ensure hygiene. Pfund also produced Germany's first condensed milk and sold various dairy products, including Bulgarian yogurt and milkshakes.

The company was expropriated by the East German government in 1972 and ceased production in 1979. The shop reopened in 1995. Today, this dairy shop is famous for its original hand-painted tiles by Villeroy & Boch and was listed by Guinness World Records in 1997 as the "Most Beautiful Dairy Shop in the World." If you visit, be sure to enjoy a glass of pure, rich milk!

## The Golden Horseman of Dresden (Goldener Reiter)
![Goldener Reiter]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Reiter.jpg)
As early as 1704, Augustus the Strong commissioned a sculptor to design an equestrian statue. After years of effort, the statue was cast between 1732 and 1734 and officially unveiled in November 1736.

Having weathered many storms and several restorations, including damage during WWII, the monument was re-erected in 1956 as part of Dresden's 750th-anniversary celebrations. It was regilded in 1965, restoring it to its former glory.

Augustus the Strong, of the House of Wettin, served as the Prince-Elector of Saxony from 1694 and was the King of Poland and Grand Duke of Lithuania from 1697–1706 and 1709–1733.

## Dresden Culture Palace (Kulturpalast)
![Kulturpalast]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Soviet.jpg)
Anyone living in Dresden will surely recognize the Kulturpalast, as it sits directly opposite the Altmarkt—it’s hard to miss!

Passing by the other day, I was surprised to find that it still preserves Soviet-era mosaic murals! Similar to other Soviet mosaics, these murals depict themes of unity and cooperation among people from various fields. Right in the center is a representative of the Soviet proletariat—a farmer holding a sickle. It’s truly remarkable!

Design for the Kulturpalast began in the 1950s, and it opened in 1969. Originally, there were plans to build a massive Stalinist-style structure similar to Moscow's "Seven Sisters." Unfortunately, this never materialized, and even the planned third floor and parade stands were never built. If you have the chance to pass by, take a moment to stop and appreciate this Soviet-style art!

## Saxon Switzerland (Sächsische Schweiz)
![Sächsische Schweiz]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Dresden/DD_Bastei.jpg)
The Saxon Switzerland National Park is located along the Elbe River southeast of Dresden. It is famous for its unique Cretaceous sandstone rock formations, deep valleys, table mountains, and gorges, which are unparalleled in Central Europe. It is a renowned destination for tourism and rock climbing, with the most famous landmarks being the Bastei and the Bastei Bridge (Bastei Brücke).

The Bastei was first mentioned in 1592 (then called Pastey) and was highly recommended in Carl Heinrich Nicolai's 19th-century travel guide. As the number of visitors grew, the Bastei Bridge was rebuilt from wood into a stone bridge 76 meters long and 40 meters high, becoming a must-visit spot today (though it requires a bit of a hike to reach).

From here, you can also see Königstein Fortress (Festung Königstein) located just across the Elbe. The oldest records of Königstein Fortress date back to 1233, mentioning King Wenceslaus I of Bohemia (Václav I). Since its completion, the fortress has never been conquered and often served as a refuge for royalty and nobles during times of war in the Middle Ages.
