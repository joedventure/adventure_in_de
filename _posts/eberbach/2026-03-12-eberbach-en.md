## The History of Eberbach
Eberbach can be translated into several names. Phonetically, it is Eberbach, but if you want something a bit more playful, you might call it "Boar Brook Town." For a more formal or grander title, it is also known as "The Staufer City of Eberbach" (Stauferstadt Eberbach).

Eberbach is located at the southern edge of the Odenwald forest along the banks of the Neckar River. Its founding was closely tied to the power struggles of the time. Around the year 1000, this area sat at the intersection of the three major bishoprics of Worms, Lorsch, and Würzburg, making it a highly contested strategic location. Before the birth of the city itself, the land was ruled by the von Lauffen family from Lauffen am Neckar (south of Heilbronn). They built a castle on the hill where Eberbach stands today, which was expanded over time by others into a defensive complex consisting of three fortresses. Today, these fortresses lie in ruins but remain historical witnesses to the town's past.

In 1227, King Henry VII (König Heinrich VII) of the House of Hohenstaufen began establishing the town below Eberbach Castle to consolidate the Hohenstaufen family's influence in the Neckar Valley. This new city served not only as an administrative center but also fulfilled military defense functions, becoming one of the key strongholds of the Hohenstaufen dynasty.

However, with the decline of the Hohenstaufen dynasty in the mid-13th century, control of Eberbach repeatedly passed into the hands of various nobles. From 1297, the Counts of Katzenelnbogen (Grafen von Katzenelnbogen) from present-day Hesse took over the city. By the early 14th century, their successors, the Lords of Weinsberg (Herren von Weinsberg), became the new rulers. In 1330, Eberbach and its surrounding areas were transferred to the Counts Palatine. From then on, the town was no longer just a stronghold on the Neckar but became an important bridgehead for the expansion of the Palatinate to the east. After the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, Eberbach became part of the Grand Duchy of Baden.

Since the Hohenstaufen dynasty is regarded as the founder of Eberbach, 1227 is recognized as its founding year. In the upcoming year of 2027, Eberbach will celebrate its 800th anniversary, an event sure to attract many history enthusiasts and tourists alike.

## Eberbach Castle Ruins (Burgruine Eberbach)
The Eberbach Castle ruins consist of three separate castles: the Front Castle (Vorderburg), the Middle Castle (Mittelburg), and the Rear Castle (Hinterburg). Among them, the Front Castle is the oldest structure, built around 1011, with the first documentary mention dating back to 1196. The core of the entire castle complex is the Middle Castle, constructed around 1200. Its magnificent main tower was once high enough to exchange signals with the Dilsberg Fortress (Feste Dilsberg) 14 kilometers away. The Rear Castle was completed around 1240 under the direction of King Henry VII, the founder of the city of Eberbach.

The fate of the castle was sealed when Count Palatine Rupert III (Rupprecht III) pledged Eberbach Castle and the town to the knight Hans von Hirschhorn. Hans viewed Eberbach Castle as a threat to his own castles in Hirschhorn and Zwingenberg; consequently, in 1403, he ordered the complete destruction of Eberbach Castle. This once glorious castle lay abandoned for five centuries until archaeological teams began excavating the ruins of the Front and Middle castles between 1908–1909 and 1927–1928, reconstructing parts of the structures.

## The Churches of Eberbach
![Eberbach_Kirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Eberbach/Eberbach_Kirche.jpg)
When visiting a German town, the churches are undoubtedly must-see attractions. There are two main churches in Eberbach. Although they were built in the 19th century, both were constructed upon the foundations of much earlier churches.

The first is the Protestant Michaelskirche, built between 1835 and 1841 to replace a predecessor completed in 1426. The other is the Catholic St. Johannes Nepomuk Kirche, built between 1884 and 1887. Its predecessor was mentioned as early as 1469, and the church we see today is the fourth structure to stand on this site.

Due to the shifting beliefs brought by the Reformation, Elector Palatine Johann Wilhelm II decided in his 1705 religious declaration that in areas with two or more churches, they should be allocated according to different faiths. Thus, the Protestants received the church located within the city center, while the Catholics held the church located outside the city walls. This arrangement can still be seen in many areas that formerly belonged to the Palatinate. Therefore, when you get off at Eberbach train station, you immediately see St. Johannes Nepomuk Kirche, while you must walk into the Old Town to find Michaelskirche nestled among the streets.

## Eberbach Old Town
![Eberbach Altstadt]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Eberbach/Eberbach_Altstadt.jpg)
Eberbach's Old Town is not very large; you can essentially explore it all in about half an hour if you walk briskly. However, you can still find some interesting sights here! The Boar Fountain in the Old Market Square is one such example 🐖🐗.

On the other side of Eberbach's Old Market Square is the historic Hotel Zum Karpfen. The family that owns the "Carp Hotel" originally started in the timber trade. Due to the owner's hospitality, more and more people began to visit, leading to the construction of the hotel in 1804. The sgraffito murals on the walls were added after 1934 at the suggestion of a friend of the owner, primarily depicting the history of Eberbach. The timber-framed building next to the hotel is one of the oldest houses in Eberbach. Currently, both buildings are listed as cultural heritage sites of Eberbach.
