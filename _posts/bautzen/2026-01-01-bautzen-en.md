## City History of Bautzen
Bautzen (Upper Sorbian: Budyšin) is often considered the informal historical capital of the Upper Lusatia (Oberlausitz) region, located at the tri-point of Germany, Poland, and the Czech Republic. It is also one of the most important cultural centers for the Sorbian people (the other being Cottbus in Lower Lusatia/Niederlausitz).

According to historical records, Thietmar von Merseburg first mentioned Bautzen's Ortenburg in 1002, referring to it as "budusin civitatem" and identifying it as the center of Upper Lusatia. During the Middle Ages, Bautzen fell under the rule of various kingdoms. For instance, in 1018, the Peace of Bautzen was signed between Holy Roman Emperor Henry II and Polish Duke Bolesław I, placing the town under Polish rule for a period. In 1031, Bautzen briefly returned to the Holy Roman Empire before being ceded to the Duchy of Bohemia in 1081, where it remained until officially joining the Electorate of Saxony in 1635.

In more recent history, a darker chapter began in 1933 when the Nazi government came to power, leading to the imprisonment of many political prisoners here. Conditions did not improve after World War II; the East German government continued to use the facilities for prisoners under the direct administration of the Stasi (State Security Service).

Bautzen's most famous specialty is Bautz’ner Mustard, which can be found almost everywhere in Germany!

The production of this mustard dates back to 1866 by the company Britze & Söhne. During the East German era in 1953, the company was nationalized. Despite the challenges following the fall of the Berlin Wall, Bautz’ner Mustard has continued to thrive thanks to its loyal fan base. Next time you enjoy a German bratwurst, be sure to try their famous "Senf mittelscharf" (medium-hot mustard) 😋.
