## History of Lorsch
Lorsch is situated within the Rhine, Main, and Neckar river basins, and its history is deeply intertwined with the Abbey of Lorsch.

In 764, during the reign of King Pippin the Short of the Frankish Kingdom, Count Cancor of the Robertians and his mother Williswinda founded a monastery in Lorsch and invited monks led by the Archbishop of Metz to settle there. Only eight years later, this monastery was elevated to a Royal Abbey, placed directly under the jurisdiction of the King of the Franks and granted the right to freely elect its abbot, gaining significant status.

In 774, the newly completed abbey church was consecrated by the Archbishop of Mainz in the presence of Charlemagne. As a generous patron, Charlemagne provided the Lorsch Abbey with vast lands and tax privileges. The Codex Laureshamensis records as many as 3,800 donations. This led to the rapid rise of Lorsch Abbey, allowing it to break free from the control of the Archdioceses of Mainz and Worms and further consolidate its position.

Under the reign of King Henry IV in 1076, Lorsch Abbey was granted minting and market rights, significantly strengthening its economic power. With the accumulation of wealth, the abbey developed into a center for medieval science and art, housing one of the most important libraries in Europe at the time. Today, many precious manuscripts are scattered across major European libraries, with the Vatican Library being one of them.

However, as the Investiture Controversy between the Holy Roman Emperor and the Pope intensified, the lands of Lorsch Abbey were gradually annexed by local nobles. Ultimately, in 1232, Emperor Frederick II placed the abbey under the jurisdiction of the Archbishop of Mainz, after which its influence began to wane.

In 1461, due to a succession dispute regarding the Archbishop of Mainz, Mainz ceded Lorsch to the Electoral Palatinate (Kurpfalz) to gain its support. However, the Electoral Palatinate embraced Protestantism and actively promoted the Reformation. Consequently, Lorsch Abbey was forced to dissolve in 1556, ending its mission as a religious institution.

While the history of the abbey paused there, in 1623, Catholic Spanish troops reclaimed Lorsch during the Thirty Years' War, returning the land to the Electorate of Mainz until its dissolution in 1803. Subsequently, Lorsch was incorporated into the Landgraviate of Hesse-Darmstadt and eventually became part of the modern German state of Hesse.

Although the active history of Lorsch Abbey has passed, its cultural legacy remains a testament to the brilliance of the European Middle Ages. For this reason, UNESCO designated the Lorsch Abbey as a World Heritage Site in 1991.

## The King's Hall (Königshalle)
![Königshalle]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Lorsch/Lorsch.jpg)
The King's Hall is one of the best-preserved ruins of Lorsch Abbey. There have been various speculations regarding its construction date. One theory suggests it was built during the era of Charlemagne; however, based on recent archaeological evidence, it is believed to have been constructed in the late 9th century, possibly linked to Louis the German, King of East Francia, who is buried here. The purpose of the King's Hall is also a subject of debate, though the most plausible theory today is that it served as a venue for official ceremonies held by rulers.

The King's Hall possesses immense artistic value. On the interior walls of the second floor are numerous Carolingian-era frescoes, and on the exterior walls and columns, one can still see the original appearance from over a thousand years ago.

As for the abbey itself, it was largely destroyed during the Thirty Years' War (1621). The existing remains were once part of the three arcades on the west side of the abbey's nave.

## Codex Laureshamensis
The Codex Laureshamensis is a vital document for studying the history and geography of Central Europe from the 8th to the 12th centuries. Written at the end of the 12th century, it is divided into two parts: the first part is a chronicle of the abbey itself, recording significant events between 1170 and 1175; the second part is a cartulary, detailing the land holdings of Lorsch Abbey at the time, covering regions from the modern-day Netherlands to Switzerland. The codex contains over 3,800 documents and mentions more than 1,000 cities in southern and western Germany for the first time. Its detailed dating provides essential information for determining the founding eras of these cities. Since the original texts have all been lost, the Codex Laureshamensis serves as a precious source for geographical and local historical research.

The codex consists of 229 parchment pages, with each page divided into two columns and written in fine Gothic minuscule. Each entry is separated by a red heading, summarizing key points of land transfers, such as dates and names, to facilitate quick retrieval. The decoration of the book is minimalist, with only a red vine ornament on a blue background on the first page; the rest of the book is almost entirely without illustration. Currently, the Codex Laureshamensis is kept at the State Archives in Würzburg, and an online version is also available: doi.org/10.11588/diglit.19939
