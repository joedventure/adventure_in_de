If you want to understand the history of Koblenz, head straight to Görresplatz! The entire city's past is encapsulated in the monument known as the Historiensäule (History Column).

This sculpture, approximately ten meters high, was created by Jürgen Weber and completed in 1992 to commemorate the 2000th anniversary of Koblenz's founding. Through ten detailed scenes, it guides us from the Roman era, through the Middle Ages, right up to the present day.

## History of Koblenz
***1st to 5th Century: Roman Settlement***

Koblenz is situated at the magnificent confluence of the Rhine and Mosel rivers. Archaeological evidence shows that human activity here dates back to the Stone Age. On the high ground of Ehrenbreitstein, the Celtic Treveri tribe once built significant fortifications to protect the settlements at the foot of the mountain.

Between approximately 100 and 44 BC, the Romans gradually expanded into this region. To consolidate new territories and secure cities along the Rhine, they built a wooden fort at the junction of the two rivers around 10 BC, calling it castellum apud Confluentes (meaning "fort at the confluence"). This name is believed to be the origin of the name "Koblenz."

Over the next 500 years, a settlement developed alongside the fort, inhabited by merchants, artisans, and families of soldiers, allowing Koblenz to grow into a vibrant town.

***6th to 9th Century: Royal Estates and Assembly Center***

Around 470 AD, the Franks took control of Koblenz. Due to its superior strategic location, Koblenz became part of the royal property; the Frankish King Childebert II visited in 585 AD. Furthermore, Koblenz became a frequent meeting place for high-level political figures. The Treaty of Verdun, a pivotal moment in history, was negotiated at the Basilica of St. Castor (Basilika St. Kastor). This treaty led to the division of the Frankish Empire in the following decades, forming the early boundaries of modern Western European nations. Despite this, Koblenz never became the central city of the kingdom.

***10th to 12th Century: From the Frankish Empire to the Electorate of Trier***

In 925 AD, after the Lotharingia region (including the Archbishopric of Trier) was incorporated into East Francia, the Archbishop of Trier began to show a keen interest in the area between the two rivers. In the early 9th century, Archbishop Hetti of Trier received patronage from King Louis the Pious (Ludwig der Fromme) to establish the Basilica of St. Castor at the confluence. In 1018, Emperor Henry II (Kaiser Heinrich II.) granted the rights of Koblenz, including customs and minting, to the Archbishopric of Trier. From then on, Koblenz remained under the rule of the Prince-Electors of Trier for nearly eight centuries (until 1794), during which the city's infrastructure was established. Meanwhile, centers for trade and handicrafts emerged, along with the earliest urban buildings and markets.

***12th to 13th Century: The Crusades and Slave Trade***

In the Middle Ages, Koblenz was located on one of Europe's most important trade routes. Whether it was logistics along the Rhine or pilgrims and crusaders passing through, everyone had to cross this point. Many local monasteries and hospitals provided accommodation for these travelers, and local markets benefited from the increasing foot traffic. Thus, although Koblenz was not the starting point of the Crusades, the city's development was deeply influenced by them. The oldest surviving customs registers in Koblenz even mention a slave tax, suggesting that these slaves were spoils of war.

***13th to 16th Century: A Prosperous City***

Between the 13th and 16th centuries, Koblenz developed into a thriving city. Handicrafts and commerce flourished, while monasteries, churches, and the administration of the Electorate of Trier shaped daily life. Relics from this period remain well-preserved, including medieval churches, the Old Castle (Alte Burg) on the banks of the Mosel, and the Mosel Bridge (now the Balduinbrücke) built by Elector Balduin von Luxemburg.

With economic prosperity came increased civic confidence. Guilds were formed, and calls for public participation in government grew louder. New districts rose, markets boomed, and city walls were expanded. Gothic architecture dominated the landscape, accompanied by new religious movements and monastic scholarship. Koblenz became a center of medieval trade and cultural life.

***17th-18th Century: The Thirty Years' War and Witch Hunts***

During the Thirty Years' War, French and Holy Roman Empire forces fought fiercely for control of Koblenz. The reconstruction efforts following the war were once again wiped out during the War of the Palatine Succession. Although Koblenz was not captured, it suffered heavy damage from French artillery, and the castles of Stolzenfels and Lahneck to the south were destroyed.

Additionally, this period was fraught with religious fear, social tension, famine, and disease. Under the influence of the Counter-Reformation, several witch trials took place, marking a dark chapter in the city's history.

***Circa 1800: The French Revolution***

With the completion of the Electoral Palace (Kurfürstliches Schloss), Koblenz became a royal residence. However, the turmoil of the French Revolution soon spread here. In 1791-92, numerous French nobles fled to Koblenz for refuge, and Archbishop Clemens Wenceslaus accepted many exiled members of the French royal family, earning the city the nickname "Little Paris." Under the pressure of the unstoppable French army, the Archbishop was forced to flee in 1794. The city surrendered peacefully, and French troops occupied Koblenz, eventually annexing it into France until Russian troops took the city in 1814. Notably, Napoleon and his wife Josephine visited Koblenz in 1804.

During the French occupation, French law, modern administration, and the metric system were introduced. Secularization policies led to the dissolution of church institutions and the nationalization of their property. The political changes brought by French rule profoundly influenced Koblenz into the modern era.

***19th-20th Century: The Prussian Period***

The politician Metternich, who hailed from Koblenz, played a decisive role in the reorganization of Europe after the Napoleonic Wars. At the Congress of Vienna in 1815, the Rhineland and Westphalia were ceded to Prussia, and Koblenz became the seat of the Oberpräsident (High President) of the Rhine Province. From 1817, the Ehrenbreitstein Fortress began construction, becoming one of the strongest bastions in the west of the kingdom.

Throughout the 19th century, Koblenz's development was shaped by its military and administrative roles, which somewhat limited industrial growth. However, shipping trade on the Rhine and tourism flourished. For example, Queen Victoria of the United Kingdom toured the Rhine by boat in 1845 and stayed at the romantic Stolzenfels Castle.

***The World Wars: Destruction of the City***

During World War I, Koblenz was a key strategic location for the German army heading to the Western Front. After the war, the entire Rhineland was briefly occupied by Allied forces until it returned to German control in 1936. During World War II, 87% of Koblenz's urban area was destroyed in air raids, and the city was occupied by US forces in March 1945. From July of the same year, the city became part of the French occupation zone.

After the war, following the resolutions of the Potsdam Conference, American and French forces stationed in Koblenz facilitated rapid reconstruction. On August 30, 1946, the state of Rhineland-Palatinate (Rheinland-Pfalz) was created here, with Koblenz as its capital until it moved to Mainz in May 1950.

***Today: A Future-Oriented City***

In 1948, the Rittersturz Conference (Rittersturzkonferenz) held in Koblenz made significant contributions to Germany's post-war order, serving as a milestone on the road to the Basic Law. In the following decades, Koblenz developed into the largest garrison city in West Germany.

Today, Koblenz is a major economic and administrative hub. As a modern university city, it attracts talent from all over the world. The confluence of the Rhine and Mosel, the green spaces, and the vibrant urban life provide Koblenz with a high quality of life.

## The Fortifications of Koblenz
Did you know? In addition to the previously mentioned Ehrenbreitstein Fortress, the entire fortification system of Koblenz included numerous smaller forts and bastions surrounding the city. These included Feste Kaiser Franz to the north, Fort Asterstein to the southeast, Feste Kaiser Alexander to the southwest, and Fort Großfürst Konstantin. While construction times varied slightly, they were all built between 1816 and 1827, forming a formidable defensive network.

Following Germany's defeat in World War I, the Treaty of Versailles mandated the complete disarmament and demolition of military fortifications west of the Rhine and within 50 kilometers of its banks. Consequently, Koblenz was forced to dismantle its once-proud defense system. Fort Großfürst Konstantin was the only one granted an exemption due to its preservation value; its structure remained largely intact and was even topped with an anti-aircraft command center during WWII. Today, it serves as a museum and cultural venue. The other forts gradually fell into ruin and have since become suburban parks. The only surviving remnants are the Neoclassical Lion’s Gate (Löwentor) of Feste Kaiser Alexander, the unique circular design of Fort Asterstein, and the remaining foundations and tunnels of Feste Kaiser Franz.

## Deutsches Eck (German Corner)
![Deutsches Eck]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Deutsches Eck.jpg)
![Deutsches Eck2]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Deutsches Eck2.jpg)
The Deutsches Eck is arguably one of Koblenz's most famous landmarks. Standing here, you can feel the majestic power of the confluence of the Rhine and Moselle rivers. However, the "German Corner" was not originally located here, and its history is deeply intertwined with the Teutonic Knights.

It all began in 1216 AD when Archbishop Theoderich von Wied of Trier summoned the Teutonic Knights to Koblenz, granting them part of the lands of St. Castor's Monastery and St. Nicholas Hospital. The knights established a settlement at the confluence of the two rivers, calling it "Deutscher Ordt" (German Place). This name soon evolved into "Deutsches Eck." Its location shifted from the original Teutonic settlement to the current monument site following the completion of the memorial to Emperor Wilhelm I in 1897.

As for the monument itself, it was commissioned by Emperor Wilhelm II to honor his grandfather, Wilhelm I, for unifying the German states and founding the German Empire. The monument was destroyed during WWII, and it wasn't until 1993 that the reconstructed statue was returned to its base. The monument stands 37 meters tall, featuring a 14-meter equestrian statue of Emperor Wilhelm I accompanied by a genius (guardian spirit).

## Deutschherrenhaus (Teutonic Order Building)
![Deutschherrenhaus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Deutschherrenhaus.jpg)
As mentioned earlier, the Teutonic Order (Deutscher Orden) established a foothold in Koblenz in 1216 at the invitation of Archbishop Theoderich von Wied. This was their first settlement in the Rhine region. By the mid-13th century, it was elevated to a bailiwick (Ballei) overseeing branches in Mainz, Cologne, and elsewhere. In the 15th century, it became one of the four chamber bailiwicks (Kammerballeien) directly subordinate to the Grand Master of the Order, underscoring its historical importance.

As the site developed, several buildings were erected. In addition to St. Nicholas Hospital, the knights built several chapels and a commander’s residence. Due to the ravages of war, only ruins of the chapels remain scattered on the site. The commander's residence, however, still stands today as the Deutschherrenhaus.

This building has served various purposes over the centuries: originally the residence for the Teutonic commander, it was leased for private use after 1800. When Prussia took over the Rhine region, it was converted into a military supply depot and later became the State Archives in 1895. Since 1992, the restored building has housed the Ludwig Museum, which focuses on contemporary art, particularly French works. This is why you will find "Le Pouce"—a giant thumb sculpture by French artist César Baldaccini—right in front of the building.

## Le Pouce de César
![Koblenz Thumb]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Thumb.jpg)
After seeing that massive thumb at the Ludwig Museum in Koblenz, I did some research on César Baldaccini—his work is incredibly fascinating!

César Baldaccini was a master of Nouveau Réalisme, spending his life transforming the waste of modern society into art. Initially, he used scrap metal to create images of insects and monsters. Later, he began using hydraulic presses to crush cars into compact blocks—an extreme creative method that became one of his signatures. In the later stages of his career, his style took a 180-degree turn; instead of compressing objects, he created monumental versions of them, such as the thumb in Koblenz. However, the truly staggering version of "Le Pouce" is in Paris, standing a full 12 meters high!

Additionally, the César Award—often called the "French Oscars"—is named after him, and the trophy itself is a classic "compressed" gold bar designed by the artist.

## Basilica of St. Castor (Basilika St. Kastor)
![St. Kastor]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Basilika St. Kastor.jpg)
The Basilica of St. Castor is the oldest church in Koblenz. Originally a Roman temple, the site was converted into a Christian cemetery in the 5th century. Construction of the church we see today began in the 9th century, commissioned by Archbishop Hetti of Trier at the request of King Louis the Pious of the Frankish Kingdom, and was completed in 836.

This church holds significant political importance in European history. In 842 AD, negotiations held here by the sons of Louis the Pious led to the signing of the Treaty of Verdun the following year. This treaty divided the Frankish Kingdom into three parts, laying the territorial foundations for the future Holy Roman Empire and France. Furthermore, it long served as a meeting place for emperors and kings to mediate disputes; for instance, Conrad III was elected king here in 1138.

Over the centuries, the church's architecture has undergone numerous transformations. Between 1160 and 1208, it was rebuilt into the three-aisled Romanesque basilica seen today, with the addition of its grand twin towers. Gothic elements were introduced after the 15th century, including the installation of exquisite star vaulting, considered a masterpiece of medieval architecture. Although the church lost its collegiate status in 1802 due to the secularization policies of the French government and suffered heavy damage from air raids and shelling during World War II, its post-war reconstruction has made its historical value even more precious.

## Castor Fountain (Kastorbrunnen)
When you walk to the plaza on the west side of the Basilica of St. Castor, take a close look at the Castor Fountain—you will find a story full of historical irony.

In 1812, the French prefect Jules Doazan had an inscription carved on the fountain to commemorate Napoleon's anticipated victory in the Russian campaign. However, Napoleon was ultimately defeated. When the Russian commander took control of Koblenz in 1814 and saw this inscription, he displayed a brilliant sense of humor. Rather than erasing the original text, he added a sentence below it in French: "Seen and approved by us, Russian Commandant of the city of Koblenz." It’s basically the "Threads" of that era! 😆

Here is the original text for those interested:

«An mdcccxii / Mémorable par la campagne contre les Russes / sous le Préfectura de Jules Doazan.»

«Vu et approuvé par nous Commandant / russe de la ville de Coblentz / le 1er janvier 1814.»

## St. Florin's Church (Florinkirche)
![Florinkirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Florinkirche.jpg)
Built around 1100 AD, St. Florin's Church appears modest on the outside, but its history has been quite turbulent. It was twice struck by lightning and burned down, and it suffered severe damage from French troops during the War of the Palatine Succession in 1688, only being fully restored by 1708.

The most absurd crisis occurred during the Napoleonic occupation. The French took a liking to the church's high ceilings and came up with a bizarre plan to convert it into a butcher shop, envisioning meat hanging from the church vaults and stalls set up inside. Fortunately, after Prussia took over Koblenz in 1814, the plan was abandoned. It’s hard to imagine such a scene ever taking place.

If you have the chance to visit St. Florin's, be sure to look up. Embedded in one of the church vaults is an original cannonball with a handle. This isn't modern art; the cannonball was intentionally preserved to commemorate the destruction the city suffered in 1688.

Additionally, the interior features precious stained glass from 1300 and the early 19th century, as well as 12th-13th century Gothic murals accidentally discovered during restoration in 1970. Don't miss these unique details!

## Electoral Palace (Kurfürstliches Schloss)
![Schloss]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Koblenz/Koblenz_Kurfürstliches Schloss.jpg)
Situated right on the banks of the Rhine, the Electoral Palace was built between 1777 and 1793 by Clemens Wenzeslaus, the Prince-Elector of Trier. It was one of the last palaces built in Germany before the outbreak of the French Revolution and is the most important example of early French Classicism in southwestern Germany. Between 1850 and 1858, it served as the residence for the Prussian Prince William (later Emperor William I) and his wife Augusta, adding a strong royal character to the palace.

The palace was severely destroyed during World War II, at one point leaving only the outer walls, but restoration was completed by 1951. Today, the Electoral Palace houses various federal government offices and provides space for events, while its gardens are a popular spot for citizens to take a stroll.

## Koblenz Old Town (Koblenz Altstadt)
Walking through the Koblenz Old Town, the charming alleys and historic houses often make visitors stop to admire the view. Among them, the most famous cluster of old houses is located at the intersection of Am Plan, Löhrstraße, Marktstraße, and Altengraben, known as the "Four Towers" (Vier Türme).

The first building is called "Zum grünen Baum" (Under the Green Tree). There is a pedestrian passage at the corner of the house, where trams operating from 1899 to 1967 used to turn.

The second building is "Zum heiligen Petrus" (House of St. Peter), which served as a pharmacy from 1730 until the early 21st century.

The third building is the "Hauptwache" (Main Guardhouse). Until 1848, it was the primary guardroom for Koblenz, where soldiers of the Electorate of Trier performed their police duties.

The fourth building has no name, but it survived World War II intact and was restored in 1778.

Since 2002, the Four Towers have been listed as historical monuments.
